SUNetID

You should have a SUNetID. If you are a continuing Stanford student, you probably already have an ID. If you are a new Stanford student, you can create your account here. If you are a non-Stanford intern, please ask Prof. Pavone to create a SUNetID for you following the instructions here. He will need your full name, date of birth and your e-mail address.

Safety training

Before you can enter the lab, you should complete the steps listed on this Google doc. Note: minors are not allowed to obtain lab access. They should still complete the safety courses and the lab walkthrough. For more information on policies for minors, click here.

Desks in the ASL

We strive to provide desk space in the lab for students doing a rotation with the ASL, although this is not always possible. The desks in Room 09A are reserved for 290 students and visiting researchers.

ASL IT: SVN, GitHub, FTP, lab computers, mailing lists, Slack

  • The lab uses a SVN server (Segovia) to share papers, reports, and the shared lab bibliography (more on this later). Ask one of the ASL sysadmins, which is Robert Dyro (rdyro@stanford.edu) at the time of writing, to create an user ID for you. If you are not a core member, please ask your project leader what folders you will need access to.
  • The lab's code lives in the ASL Github account. Ask Ed (schmrlng) for access.
  • Subscribe to the stanford-asl mailing list here.
    • For more information about the ASL's IT infrastructure, see here.
  • We have an ASL Slack for general, unofficial discussion. Ask any lab member to be invited. There is no expectation that you monitor it, but you may find useful discussion here.

LaTeX and bibliography

  • The ASL is strictly a LaTeX shop: all of our papers are written in LaTeX. You may write non-collaborative documents (e.g. your 290 report) in any software you like - however, if you turn it into a conference paper, you will have to rewrite it in LaTeX, so why not start the right way? We recommend this short guide for absolute LaTeX beginners - it's easier than you think. Papers are shared on SVN.
  • All collaborative documents should use the shared ASL bibliography. The bibliography is in /bib/main.bib on Segovia, the lab's SVN server. Please familiarize yourself with the bibliography guidelines at /bib/Stanford ASL Bib Standards v[currentversion].pdf .

Technical Writing Guide

    Since skillful writing is a major part of being a successful researcher, new students should read the following document which includes excerpts from a widely-used technical writing textbook:

    Technical Writing Guide PDF

    It lays out a series of clear, practical guidelines for everything from word choice to sentence and paragraph structure, which make an enormous impact on the quality and readability of technical works. Having a clear set of writing principles saves time and will improve productivity for everyone involved on your future papers/proposals!

Paper Reviewing Guide

    Peer review is part of the backbone of academic research and can provide great value both to those under review and to the reviewers themselves. If you are a minor

  • Unfortunately, you will not be provided with an access code to the lab, due to Stanford policy.
  • Before you obtain a SUNetID, you and your mentor should complete all the steps outlined on this page. In particular, the mentor should register the internship with Stanford and the minor should complete the Assumption of Risk and Medical Information forms.
  • After Prof. Pavone signs the lab-specific walkthrough form, send a digital copy to the lab safety coordinator, Joe Lorenzetti (jlorenze@stanford.edu).
